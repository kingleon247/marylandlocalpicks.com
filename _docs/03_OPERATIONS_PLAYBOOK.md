# Maryland Local Picks — Operations Playbook


> **Document role:** Repeatable workflow for selling, producing, mailing, reporting, and renewing each card.
>
> **Current locked offer (live site):** Premium shared 9x12 postcard mailed to **10,000 local households**. Firm prices: Half Spot $350, Standard Spot $600 (recommended), Double Spot $1,100. USPS/logistics sections referencing 2,500/5,000 runs need manual recomputation for 10,000.

## Page 3: How to Sell, Build, Print, Mail, Report, and Renew Each Local Card

### 1. Purpose of This Playbook

This document explains how Maryland Local Picks should operate each print-and-digital local advertising edition from start to finish.

The goal is to create a repeatable system that can eventually be handed to a team.

This playbook covers:

* city and route selection
* edition planning
* advertiser prospecting
* sales workflow
* payment collection
* ad size inventory
* business information collection
* digital landing page setup
* QR code setup
* card design
* proofing
* printing
* USPS EDDM preparation
* mailing drop-off
* advertiser reporting
* renewals
* recurring payments
* prepaid packages
* internal time estimates
* quality control

The operating principle is simple:

**Sell first, then produce.**

Maryland Local Picks should not pay for printing or postage until enough advertisers have paid to justify the mailing.

### 2. Core Operating Model

Each edition is a city or ZIP-based local advertising card connected to a digital city page.

Example:

**Catonsville Local Picks**
`marylandlocalpicks.com/catonsville`

The printed card goes to selected households through USPS Every Door Direct Mail.

The digital version remains online and includes:

* business landing pages
* local offers
* paid/free Pick of the Week features
* email opt-in
* advertiser inquiry CTA
* tracking/reporting

Each advertiser may receive:

* physical card placement
* digital business landing page
* QR code destination
* offer/coupon placement
* city directory listing
* basic tracking
* renewal option
* digital-only downsell option
* call tracking upsell
* Pick of the Week upsell
* future AI answering or website upsell

The printed card is the wedge.

The website and advertiser relationship are the long-term assets.

### 3. Recommended Mailing Cadence

The target mailing cadence is approximately **every six weeks**.

This creates about **8 mailings per year**.

A six-week cadence works because it balances:

* repetition
* urgency
* sales cycle
* design/production time
* route planning
* advertiser renewals
* freshness for residents
* enough spacing so the card does not feel constant or stale

A possible annual rhythm:

1. Late winter / early spring edition
2. Spring home services edition
3. Early summer edition
4. Mid-summer local picks edition
5. Back-to-school / early fall edition
6. Fall home prep edition
7. Holiday local picks edition
8. New year / winter services edition

The calendar should stay flexible. Local seasonality matters.

Examples:

* landscapers before spring
* HVAC before summer and winter
* roofers and gutter companies before fall
* restaurants around holidays
* fitness/wellness in January
* real estate/homeowner resources in spring and early summer
* tax/accounting in winter
* car wash/detailing in spring and summer

### 5. City and Route Selection

The first target market should be manageable.

Initial target:

**Catonsville Local Picks**

Potential ZIPs:

* 21228
* nearby surrounding routes if strategically useful

EDDM does not allow choosing an exact arbitrary number of households. You select full carrier routes, and the final quantity equals the sum of those routes.

Before selling the card, choose a likely target range:

* 10,000 local households for launch
* possibly 10,000 local households after validation
* multiple 10,000-home postcard editions if splitting by ZIP/area

A 2,500-household launch is useful because:

* lower postage
* easier sales price
* lower first-run risk
* less physical handling
* faster validation

A 5,000-household mailing is useful because:

* wider exposure
* stronger advertiser value
* better farm coverage
* stronger case for higher pricing
* better repetition if the goal is market presence

The decision should be based on:

* advertiser demand
* available routes
* cost
* desired farm coverage
* ability to sell enough spots
* ability to physically prep and drop the mail

### 6. Multiple Cards and Multiple Cities

The platform should support more than one card or city at the same time.

Possible models:

#### A. One city, one card

Example:

* Catonsville only
* 10,000 local households
* one edition every six weeks

Best for first validation.

#### B. One city, two route groups

Example:

* Catonsville East
* Catonsville West
* each around 10,000 local households

A business that wants 10,000 local households can buy placement on both cards.

#### C. Two nearby cities

Example:

* Catonsville Local Picks
* Arbutus Local Picks

This gives expansion potential, but it doubles sales work.

#### D. Multi-card regional bundle

Example:

* Catonsville
* Arbutus
* Ellicott City

A larger advertiser can buy one placement across multiple editions.

Operational rule:

A business should have one canonical business record and one landing page, but can have multiple placements across different editions.

Each placement should have its own QR short code so results can be tracked by card and edition.

### 8. Placement Inventory and Sizing

The card should support flexible placement sizes.

Suggested sizes:

* half
* single
* double
* quad

A unit system should be used internally.

Example:

* half = 1 unit
* single = 2 units
* double = 4 units
* quad = 8 units

Each edition has a total available unit capacity.

This allows the card to be filled as advertisers come in.

Example:

* one quad sponsor consumes more space
* two double sponsors consume meaningful space
* half spots allow smaller businesses to participate
* standard spots remain the standard product

The operator should monitor:

* total units available
* units sold
* units reserved
* units paid
* units remaining
* category conflicts
* premium/locked placements

No spot should be considered sold until payment is received.

* ad size
* whether digital landing page is included
* whether QR/tracking is included
* whether the advertiser wants exclusivity

A simple pricing ladder is easier to sell.

Example structure:

Do not underprice just to make selling easier. If the card requires hours of sales, design, coordination, EDDM prep, and follow-up, the profit must justify the work.

### 10. Payment Rules

Upfront payment should be required.

Rules:

1. No payment, no reserved print spot.
2. Verbal interest does not hold a spot.
3. A spot can be temporarily held only for a short period if strategically necessary.
4. Final artwork does not begin until payment is received or a payment agreement is signed.
5. Multi-mailing discounts or bonuses require payment commitment.
6. Digital-only listings should be monthly recurring.
7. Call tracking should be monthly recurring.
8. Pick of the Week can be sold one-time or as part of a package.
9. Annual packages should be prepaid or placed on automatic billing.
10. Refund policy should be clear before accepting payment.

Payment methods:

* Stripe Payment Link
* invoice
* credit/debit card
* ACH later
* check only if operationally convenient

The easiest early workflow is:

* advertiser says yes
* send payment link
* payment confirms spot
* then collect assets

### 12. Digital-Only Downsell

Digital-only is important.

Some businesses will not buy print.

Do not lose them completely.

Offer a lower-cost monthly digital option.

Includes:

* business landing page
* listing on city page
* current offer/coupon
* basic tap tracking
* optional Pick of the Week purchase
* no printed card placement

This is useful when a business says:

* “Print is too expensive.”
* “Maybe next time.”
* “We don’t have the budget.”
* “We only want digital.”
* “Can we just be on the website?”

Digital-only should also be the downgrade path.

If a print advertiser does not renew:

**“No problem. We can keep your landing page and offer active as a digital-only listing for less.”**

This keeps some revenue instead of losing the business entirely.

3. Explain the local card.
4. Show printed mockup/sample.
5. Explain household reach.
6. Explain digital landing page included.
7. Explain category/space availability.
8. Offer placement size.
9. Collect payment.
10. Collect business info.
11. Send proof.
12. Mail card.
13. Report results.
14. Ask for renewal.

The pitch should be simple.

Example:

**“I’m putting together Catonsville Local Picks. It’s a printed local card and digital guide featuring selected local businesses, offers, and places worth knowing. It goes to local households, and each business also gets a digital landing page with a QR code. I’m keeping it limited by category so it doesn’t turn into a cluttered coupon sheet.”**

For local business owners, the strongest framing is:

* local
* community
* limited spots
* selected businesses

* no complicated setup

### 22. USPS EDDM Workflow

Every mailing should be prepared using the current USPS EDDM tool and rules.

High-level workflow:

1. Choose target ZIP/city.
2. Select carrier routes.
3. Review route counts.
4. Confirm total pieces required.
5. Confirm drop-off Post Office.
6. Generate paperwork.
7. Print postage statement and facing slips.
8. Separate mailpieces by route.
9. Bundle according to USPS requirements.
10. Attach facing slips.
11. Drop off at Post Office.
12. Pay postage or bring proof of online payment.
13. Get receipt/acceptance confirmation.

Important notes:

* EDDM is route-based.
* The total mailing quantity is based on selected routes.
* You cannot simply choose exactly 2,500 or 5,000 unless routes add up that way.
* Retail EDDM has a daily limit per ZIP.
* The working planning rate should be verified before every mailing.
* Mailpieces must comply with USPS flat-size and weight requirements.
* Bundling and facing slips matter. Bad prep can delay or reject the drop.

### 23. EDDM Physical Prep

When printed cards arrive:

1. Inspect the shipment.
2. Count or estimate total pieces.
3. Separate by selected route counts.
4. Create bundles for each route.
5. Use bundle sizes allowed by USPS.
6. Place facing slip on top of each bundle.
7. Mark number of pieces per bundle if required.
8. Keep route paperwork organized.
9. Load by route or Post Office.
10. Bring tape/rubber bands/straps as needed.

Supplies to have:

* rubber bands or straps
* boxes/totes
* printed route forms
* printed facing slips
* Sharpie
* packing tape
* clipboard
* receipt folder
* phone charger
* dolly/cart if needed

For 2,500 cards, expect physical handling but manageable work.

For 5,000 cards, expect heavier lifting, more bundles, more time, and more need for organization.

### 27. Renewal Workflow

Renewal should begin before the next card is being sold out.

Renewal timing:

* first contact: 1–2 weeks after mailing
* second contact: 3–4 weeks after mailing
* final renewal deadline: before design lock

Renewal pitch:

**“I’m starting the next Catonsville Local Picks edition. Since you were on the last one, I wanted to give you first option to keep your spot before I open it to another business in your category.”**

If they hesitate:

Offer:

* smaller size
* digital-only listing
* Pick of the Week
* different offer
* different route/ZIP
* prepaid package
* category/ZIP lock if they want consistency

Do not let the renewal conversation become only about immediate response.

Reframe around:

* repetition
* local awareness
* digital presence
* QR traffic
* category protection
* being seen consistently
* community presence

### 28. Rotation and Freshness

The card should not be identical every time.

Ways to keep it fresh:

* rotate seasonal advertisers
* rotate restaurants/food offers
* rotate Pick of the Week
* change card colors/theme by season
* include new categories
* include limited-time offers
* switch some businesses by ZIP/route
* include a few new local picks each edition

However, some placements should stay consistent.

Lock or repeat:

* annual advertisers
* category-exclusive advertisers
* major sponsors
* Realtor/farming placement
* high-value referral relationships
* businesses paying for ZIP/route lock

The goal is balance:

**Fresh enough for residents. Consistent enough for advertisers.**
